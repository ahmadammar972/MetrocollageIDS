What is Data Science?

An interdisciplinary field that uses algorithms, procedures, and processes to examine large amounts of data to uncover hidden patterns and generate insights.

To create prediction models, data scientists use advanced machine learning algorithms to sort through, organize and learn from structured and unstructured data.

Objective

To guide decision making and strategic planning through the analysis of large data sets.

Data Science Combines:

➕ Math & Statistics
💻 Specialized Programming
🧠 Advanced Analytics & AI / ML
🏢 Specific Subject Matter Expertise

Java in Data Science

Java is a strongly-typed, object-oriented language that remains a cornerstone for enterprise-level data architecture and data engineering.

Why Java?

  • Performance: Offers excellent execution speed and scalability for complex tasks.
  • Big Data Native: Major frameworks like Hadoop and Kafka are written natively in Java/Scala.
  • Libraries: Tools like Weka, Deeplearning4j, and MOA provide strong machine learning capabilities.

Building robust, large-scale ETL (Extract, Transform, Load) pipelines and integrating machine learning models into massive, pre-existing corporate software systems.

SQL (Structured Query Language)

SQL is the absolute bedrock of data retrieval. Before you can analyze data with Python or build models with Java, you must extract it from relational databases.

query.sql
-- Retrieving user purchasing behavior
SELECT customer_id, COUNT(order_id) AS total_orders
FROM sales_data
WHERE purchase_date >= '2023-01-01'
GROUP BY customer_id
ORDER BY total_orders DESC;
Data Extraction: Pulling specific datasets from massive relational architectures (MySQL, PostgreSQL, Oracle).
Data Transformation: Cleaning, joining, and aggregating tables before exporting to tools like Pandas.

Big Data: Hadoop & Apache Spark

Big Data frameworks are designed to process, store, and analyze massive datasets that traditional databases cannot handle on a single machine.

Apache Hadoop

The original big data pioneer.

  • HDFS: Distributed file system that splits massive files across multiple servers.
  • MapReduce: Processes data by writing and reading to the disk constantly.
  • Best for: Massive, batch-processing jobs where time is less critical than scale.

Apache Spark

The modern, lightning-fast standard.

  • In-Memory: Processes data in RAM, making it up to 100x faster than Hadoop MapReduce.
  • Streaming: Excellent for real-time data streaming and instant analytics.
  • Ecosystem: Contains built-in libraries for SQL (Spark SQL) and Machine Learning (MLlib).

Origins
History Part 1

History of Data Science

The term "data science" (originally used interchangeably with “datalogy") has existed for over thirty years.

1960

The term was used initially as a substitute for computer science by Peter Naur.

1996

For the first time, the term data science is included in the title of a conference ("Data Science, Classification, and Related methods").

1997

C.F. Jeff Wu gave the inaugural lecture entitled "Statistics = Data Science?" for his appointment to the H. C. Carver Professorship at the University of Michigan.

In his conclusion, he initiated the modern usage of the term and advocated that statistics be renamed data science and statisticians be called data scientists.

FYI (Additional Reading)
Modern Era
History Part 2

History of Data Science (Continued)

2001

William S. Cleveland introduced data science as an independent discipline.

He extended the field of statistics to incorporate "advances in computing with data" in his article "Data Science: An Action Plan for Expanding the Technical Areas of the Field of Statistics."

2002

The International Council for Science: Committee on Data for Science and Technology (CODATA) started the Data Science Journal.

It focused on issues such as the description of data systems, their publication on the internet, applications, and legal issues.

2012

The Harvard Business Review published the famous article: "Data Scientist: The Sexiest Job of the 21st Century".

It asserted that a data scientist is "a new breed", and that a shortage of data scientists is becoming a serious constraint in some sectors.

Raw Data vs. Processed Data

Raw Data (Source / Atomic)

Data that has not been processed for use. It is measured and collected directly from machines, the web, etc., and is usually not in a format ready for analysis.

Processed Data

Data that has undergone cleaning and transformation. It is converted from raw data into a format that can be easily analyzed and visualized.

Real World: The Supermarket POS

A Point-of-Sale terminal collects huge volumes of raw data daily, but it yields no information until processed.

  • Processing requires: Selective extraction, organization, formatting, and analysis.
  • The Yield: What items a customer buys, when they buy them, and at what price.
  • The Action: This information is subjected to predictive technology analysis to help plan future marketing campaigns.

Rectangular Data

Rectangular data is a two-dimensional matrix where rows indicate records (cases) and columns indicate features (variables).

The Challenge

Data doesn't always start in this form. Unstructured data (like text) must be processed and manipulated so it can be represented as a set of features.

Step 1: Raw Unstructured Text

1. "I like shopping."
2. "I don't like shopping."

Step 2: Extracted Rectangular Matrix (Features)

ID T do not like shopping

What is Business Intelligence (BI)?

BI refers to the technologies, applications, strategies, and practices used to collect, integrate, analyze, and present business information. The goal of BI is to support better business decision making by transforming data into actionable insights.

Core Components of BI

1. Data Warehousing Aggregating data from multiple sources into a central repository.
2. Data Mining Using sophisticated tools to discover patterns in large datasets.
3. Reporting Providing timely reports and dashboards to stakeholders.
4. Querying Allowing users to perform ad-hoc analyses for specific questions.
5. Analytical Processing Offering capabilities for complex calculations and data modeling.

Countering the Deluge: Structural Shifts

Before analyzing data, we must fix how it is stored and shared across the organization's infrastructure.

Don't be a data hoarder

  • The Problem: Most companies collect data for years without a coherent plan, assuming they'll "do something smart with it later."
  • The Risk: Large pools of data with no indication of origin/purpose lead to wildly wrong interpretations.
  • The Cost: Storage may seem infinite in the cloud, but processing is expensive. Without efficient resources, firms hit a financial breaking point.

Dismantle your data silos

  • Ecosystem Mapping: Figure out where data resides, how it enters/leaves, and who manages it.
  • Identify Blockers: Are silos created because data is stuck in a specific SQL DB? Is there a hidden Data Lake?
  • Cultural Shift: Silos often happen passively ("If there's no benefit to my group, someone else needs to pay for it"). Break down these barriers.

Countering the Deluge: Cultural Shifts

Infrastructure is useless without a team that champions data accessibility and strict governance.

Foster a data-centric mindset

  • Universal Accessibility: High-quality data must be readily available to the right tools and the right people across all departments.
  • Proactive vs. Passive: Move away from passive data collection toward proactive, strategic usage.
  • Democratization: Empower non-technical staff with user-friendly dashboards so data isn't locked behind the IT department.

Establish top-down leadership

  • Strict Governance: Massive scale requires rigid data governance to ensure information is integrated, accurately cataloged, and trustworthy.
  • The Chief Data Officer: Many organizations are hiring CDOs to reach across isolated business units and coordinate a unified data strategy.
  • Quality Control: Establishing clear protocols for who can alter, delete, or define specific datasets.

The Bridge

The Business Analyst (BA)

A BA plays a crucial role in bridging the gap between IT and the Business side.

Primary Role

  • Understand business needs and assess the impact of changes.
  • Capture, analyze, and document requirements.
  • Communicate requirements to relevant IT/data parties.
  • Objective: Optimize and improve business operations, reduce costs, and enhance quality.

How do BAs differ from Data Roles?

Their work is less technical and highly focused on the business implications of data. They apply data findings to solve specific challenges rather than just interpreting the math. They are the translation layer between business requirements and technical specifications.
